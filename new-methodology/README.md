# REFLEX: Reflexive Equilibrium Fixed-point Learning for Endogenous Financial Markets

A machine learning framework for markets where the data distribution is not fixed but is generated by the model itself. In OTC corporate bond markets, a dealer's quoting policy reshapes future trade flow, spreads, and liquidity ‚Äî breaking the standard ML assumption that the data-generating process is independent of the learner.

REFLEX reframes learning as solving for a self-consistent equilibrium: a fixed point where the market dynamics induced by a trading policy are stable under repeated interaction with that same policy.

> **Status note.** The theory and experiments below are a research roadmap, not completed results. The literature review (18 papers, see `references.bib`) is done. The analytic boundary, the multi-dealer extension, and the calibrated phase diagrams are *target deliverables* ‚Äî none are proven or measured yet. This README is written to make that distinction explicit so nothing here is mistaken for a finished claim.

## What it does

- **Endogenous Distribution Learning** ‚Äî replaces exogenous data `D_{t+1} = P(¬∑|D_t)` with a policy-dependent system `D_{t+1} = T(D_t, œÄ_Œ∏)`.
- **Learned market response operator `T_Œ∏`** ‚Äî a differentiable, trainable operator over stochastic market transitions, replacing hand-built simulators.
- **Fixed-point objective** ‚Äî solve `(œÄ*, D*) = argmin_œÄ E_D[R(œÄ)] s.t. D = T(D, œÄ)`.
- **Stability-aware training** ‚Äî penalizes distribution collapse, liquidity fragmentation, and instability under self-induced market adaptation.
- **Implicit liquidity modeling** ‚Äî treats liquidity as a latent dynamical field induced by interaction, not an observed variable.

## Core research question

Characterize when the policy/market loop converges to a stable equilibrium vs. fails to converge, as a function of how adversarial the market is.

## Methodology

This section lays out, in order, every component of the research program: the mathematical contributions being targeted, the data this depends on, how that data will be prepared, the model architecture, and how it will be trained and tuned. ### 1. Mathematical contributions

The project's novelty claim is that the performativity stability boundary for a structural market model can be derived analytically from microstructure primitives, rather than assumed or empirically swept. This decomposes into five concrete mathematical targets, ordered by priority.

**1.1 ‚Äî Analytic stability boundary (Priority 1) ‚Äî STATUS: DONE.** The closed-form
derivation of `Œ≥`, `Œ≤`, `Œµ` and the boundary `Œµ < Œ≥/Œ≤` is complete and written up
in [`math-theory/01-analytic-stability-boundary.md`](math-theory/01-analytic-stability-boundary.md).
It reduces the performative contraction modulus `m = ŒµŒ≤/Œ≥` to the simulator's
microstructure primitives (GLFT fill-curve curvature + quoting-cost convexity ‚Üí
`Œ≥`; P&L scale ‚Üí `Œ≤`; toxic-flow feedback slope `|dœÑ/dh|` ‚Üí `Œµ`), yielding an
*a-priori* boundary plus a predict-then-verify protocol against the existing
`analysis/response_modulus.py`. Remaining sub-items below (the two extra `Œµ`
estimators and their triangulation) are the in-progress continuation of 1.1.

Target theorem: for an OTC bond market with Gu√©ant‚ÄìLehalle‚ÄìFern√°ndez-Tapia (GLFT) microstructure and Barzykin-style adverse selection, the performativity stability boundary is

```
Œµ_analytic < Œ≥(k, œÉ¬≤, q) / Œ≤(k, A, Œ¥*)
```

where `Œ≥` (strong convexity) and `Œ≤` (smoothness) are derived in closed form from the market-making value function ‚Äî not tuned ‚Äî and `Œµ` (feedback sensitivity) is derived from a perturbation expansion around adverse selection, not swept by hand.

- Derive `Œ≥` and `Œ≤` from the Avellaneda‚ÄìStoikov / GLFT exponential-intensity model `Œª(Œ¥) = A¬∑exp(‚àík¬∑Œ¥)`.
- Derive `dœÑ/dh` (the toxic-flow feedback slope) from the Barzykin et al. (2025) perturbation expansion, replacing the current heuristic `toxicity_feedback` knob.
- Triangulate `Œµ` three independent ways ‚Äî best-response (BR) slope, Sinkhorn/Wasserstein-based estimation, and the Cont‚ÄìKukanov‚ÄìStoikov informed-flow derivative ‚Äî and report agreement across all three as the evidentiary bar for the theorem.

**1.2 ‚Äî Un-blinding the operator: PerfGD correction (Priority 2) ‚Äî STATUS: DONE
(derivation).** The closed-form PerfGD correction, its convergence to the
performative optimum, its stability beyond the RRM boundary `Œµ*`, and the
echo-chamber gap are derived in
[`math-theory/02-perfgd-correction.md`](math-theory/02-perfgd-correction.md). The
correction term is `Œî = (‚àÇJ/‚àÇT)¬∑(dœÑ/dh) = ‚àíŒ≤(h‚àíœà)Œµ`, built entirely from 1.1's
closed forms (no estimation). PerfGD is governed by the objective curvature
`Œ≥_PO = Œ≥ + Œ≤Œµ(2 + c_t¬∑œà ‚àí c_t¬∑h_PO)`, not the cobweb modulus, so it converges
where RRM diverges. The remaining *code* task ‚Äî `equilibrium/perfgd_loop.py` ‚Äî is
not yet implemented.

Target theorem: under GLFT microstructure, a Performative Gradient Descent (PerfGD)‚Äìcorrected loop, using the analytic `dD/dœÜ` from 1.1, converges to the performative optimum (PO) at rate `O(1/k)` and remains stable for `Œµ` beyond the repeated-risk-minimization (RRM) boundary `Œµ*`.

- Replace blind RRM with PerfGD using the closed-form distribution-response term ‚Äî no estimation step required once 1.1 is derived.
- Quantify the "echo chamber gap" (stable-vs-optimal spread inflation), expected `O(Œµ¬≤)` near `Œµ*`, as a function of `Œµ`.

**1.3 ‚Äî Multi-dealer extension and systemic risk (Priority 3) ‚Äî STATUS: DONE
(derivation).** The closed-form lift to `N` competing dealers sharing one
informed-flow pool is derived in
[`math-theory/03-multi-dealer-systemic-risk.md`](math-theory/03-multi-dealer-systemic-risk.md).
The joint best-response Jacobian is a rank-one common-mode coupling whose unstable
eigenvalue scales by the effective dealer count `N_eff = 1 + Œ∫¬∑(N‚àí1)`, giving the
boundary `Œµ < Œ≥/(N_eff¬∑Œ≤)` (the headline `Œµ < Œ≥/(N¬∑Œ≤)` at full toxic spillover
`Œ∫ = 1`; `Œ∫ = 0` recovers the single-dealer boundary). PSNE existence/uniqueness,
joint convergence (`Œ≥_joint = Œ≥ ‚àí N_eff¬∑Œµ¬∑Œ≤`), the mean-field `N ‚Üí ‚àû` limit, and
the systemic critical dealer count `N_c = 1/m‚ÇÅ` are all derived. The remaining
*code* task ‚Äî an `N`-dealer simulator mode + common-mode BR-slope probe ‚Äî is
pending.

Target theorem: for `N` symmetric dealers, the performatively stable Nash equilibrium (PSNE) boundary generalizes to `Œµ < Œ≥/(N¬∑Œ≤)`, with joint effective curvature `Œ≥_joint ‚âà Œ≥ ‚àí N¬∑Œµ¬∑Œ≤`. Market-wide instability ‚Äî all dealers crossing the boundary simultaneously ‚Äî emerges at `N¬∑Œµ¬∑Œ≤/Œ≥ = 1`.

- Prove existence of the PSNE boundary (via performative games theory) and its convergence rate under joint variational stability (via multiplayer performative prediction theory) for `N`-dealer competition.
- Establish the mean-field limit as `N ‚Üí ‚àû`, connecting the discrete `N`-dealer game to a tractable continuum aggregate.

**1.4 ‚Äî Robust uncertainty quantification (Priority 4)**

Target result: a distributionally robust stable point ‚Äî the policy stable under worst-case `Œµ` within an ambiguity set ‚Äî with nominal boundary `Œµ*` reported alongside a robust radius `Œ¥` that shrinks at rate `O(1/‚àön)` in the number of simulated episodes `n`.

- Fit the ambiguity set from cross-seed variance in estimated `Œµ`.
- Report both nominal and robust stability boundaries; without this, a point estimate of `Œµ*` is not a statistically defensible claim.

**1.5 ‚Äî Scale and calibration (Priority 5)**

Target: extend the phase diagram from a single bond/single dealer to 100+ correlated bonds via factor-model dimensionality reduction (Bergault & Gu√©ant), with `Œ≥`/`Œ≤` calibrated from real bond characteristics (duration, DV01, spread volatility) or, absent real data, from a synthetic Cont‚ÄìKukanov‚ÄìStoikov microstructure calibration.

- Derive the dimensionality-reduction error bound as a function of residual factor variance.
- Produce a publication-grade phase diagram with tight median + IQR bands across the bond universe.

### 2. Data collection and potential sources

Two data needs are distinct and should not be conflated: (a) data to calibrate realistic microstructure parameters (`k`, `A`, `œÉ`, inventory dynamics), and (b) data/simulation needed to empirically measure `Œµ`, `m`, and the stability boundary across regimes.

**2.1 ‚Äî Calibration data (real-world microstructure)**

- **TRACE (Trade Reporting and Compliance Engine)** ‚Äî FINRA's mandatory post-trade reporting feed for U.S. corporate bonds; the standard academic source for OTC bond trade-level data (price, size, timestamp, dealer side). Available via WRDS academic access.
- **MarketAxess / Tradeweb post-trade data** ‚Äî electronic OTC trading platforms; useful for spread and liquidity metrics on traded bonds, typically licensed/paid access.
- **Bloomberg/Refinitiv terminal data** ‚Äî bond static data (duration, DV01, credit rating, issue size) needed to calibrate per-bond inventory risk parameters.
- **FINRA/MSRB academic data programs** ‚Äî possible lower-cost or free academic-tier access to trade-level OTC data; needs direct inquiry, as terms change.
- **Federal Reserve / OFR (Office of Financial Research) corporate bond liquidity datasets** ‚Äî public aggregate liquidity and spread series useful for sanity-checking calibration, though not trade-level.

**2.2 ‚Äî Simulation data (synthetic, for stability measurement)**

- Self-generated via the dealer/market simulator: each run logs `(h, œÑ)` pairs (quote width, realized toxic flow), inventory paths `q_after`, and induced order-flow distributions `D(œÜ)` across deployments.
- These logs are the raw input to every estimator in 1.1 (BR-slope, Sinkhorn distance, CKS-implied `dŒª_informed/dŒ¥`) and must be retained across the full sweep of `Œµ`, `N` (dealer count), and bond-universe size for the phase diagrams in 1.3‚Äì1.5.

**2.3 ‚Äî Open caveat**

Real trade-level OTC data (TRACE) carries licensing and access lead time; the project should explicitly decide, before experiments begin, whether the headline phase diagrams will be calibrated against real TRACE-derived parameters or run on a fully synthetic, analytically specified microstructure (acceptable for a methodology-first ICAIF/NeurIPS submission, but must be stated plainly rather than implied to be real-market validated).

### 3. Preprocessing

- **Trade data cleaning (if using TRACE or equivalent):** deduplicate reported trades (TRACE reports both sides), filter cancelled/corrected reports, exclude agency trades if isolating principal dealer risk, and align trade timestamps to a common clock across venues.
- **Spread and inventory reconstruction:** derive realized bid-ask half-spreads `h` and inventory paths `q` from sequential trade records per dealer-bond pair; this requires inferring dealer identity and side from TRACE's anonymized-but-attributable fields.
- **Parameter calibration inputs:** compute per-bond volatility `œÉ`, order-arrival intensity parameters `(A, k)` via maximum-likelihood fit of the exponential-intensity model to inter-trade times, and duration/DV01 from static bond reference data.
- **Simulator log normalization:** standardize logged `(h, œÑ, q)` tuples across seeds and runs into a consistent schema so that downstream estimators (BR-slope regression, Sinkhorn distance, CKS-implied slope) consume identical input shapes regardless of which sweep produced them.
- **Train/calibration/held-out split for any learned component of `T_Œ∏`:** split simulation episodes (not individual trades, to avoid leakage across a single equilibrium trajectory) into calibration and held-out stability-verification sets.

### 4. Model architecture

- **Market response operator `T_Œ∏`:** a differentiable, parametric operator mapping `(D_t, œÄ_Œ∏)` to `D_{t+1}` ‚Äî i.e., a learned, trainable substitute for a hand-built market simulator. Architecture choice (e.g., neural SDE, recurrent operator, or attention-based sequence model over order-flow state) is an open design decision to be settled against the stochastic-control baselines below.
- **Baseline structural policy:** the closed-form GLFT/Avellaneda‚ÄìStoikov optimal quoting policy, used both as a sanity-check baseline for `T_Œ∏` and as the source of analytically derived `Œ≥`, `Œ≤` in 1.1.
- **PerfGD correction module:** a component that estimates or consumes the analytic `dD/dœÜ` (from 1.1) and feeds a corrected gradient into the policy update ‚Äî architecturally distinct from blind RRM, which simply retrains on the latest induced distribution.
- **Multi-dealer extension:** `N` instances of the policy/operator pair interacting through a shared induced distribution, required for the systemic-risk experiments in 1.3.
- **Estimator heads (non-trained, diagnostic):** BR-slope regression, Sinkhorn-distance computation (entropic OT), and CKS-implied informed-flow slope ‚Äî these run alongside `T_Œ∏` as measurement instruments, not as part of the trained model.

### 5. Training and tuning

- **Training loop:** repeated risk minimization (RRM) as the baseline loop ‚Äî train `œÄ_Œ∏` against the current `D_t`, deploy, observe `D_{t+1} = T_Œ∏(D_t, œÄ_Œ∏)`, repeat ‚Äî with PerfGD as the corrected variant once 1.1's analytic `dD/dœÜ` is available.
- **Stability-aware loss terms:** penalties for distribution collapse, liquidity fragmentation, and divergence of the BR-slope modulus `m` away from the contraction regime (`m < 1`), in addition to the base quoting-policy loss.
- **Deploy cadence (greedy vs. lazy):** sweep the number of optimizer steps `K` taken before each redeployment (lazy deploy), since `K` is a stabilization knob ‚Äî larger `K` reduces the per-update sensitivity the optimizer sees, raising effective curvature `Œ≥_eff = Œ≥ ‚àí ŒµŒ≤`.
- **Sweep axes for the phase diagram:** feedback gain `Œµ`, number of dealers `N`, and bond-universe size, each swept across multiple seeds to produce median + IQR bands rather than single-run point estimates.
- **Hyperparameter tuning targets:** learning rate and `K` (lazy-deploy steps) for the RRM/PerfGD loop; entropic regularization strength for the Sinkhorn estimator; ambiguity-set radius for the distributionally robust stability point (1.4).
- **Validation criterion:** agreement between the three independent `Œµ` estimators (BR-slope, Sinkhorn, CKS-implied) is the primary check that the trained operator and estimators are measuring the same underlying quantity, not an artifact of one estimation method.
