# REFLEX: Reflexive Equilibrium Fixed-point Learning for Endogenous Financial Markets

A machine learning framework for markets where the data distribution is not fixed but is generated by the model itself. In OTC corporate bond markets, a dealer's quoting policy reshapes future trade flow, spreads, and liquidity — breaking the standard ML assumption that the data-generating process is independent of the learner.

REFLEX reframes learning as solving for a self-consistent equilibrium: a fixed point where the market dynamics induced by a trading policy are stable under repeated interaction with that same policy.

> **Status note.** The theory and experiments below are a research roadmap, not completed results. The literature review (18 papers, see `references.bib`) is done. The analytic boundary, the multi-dealer extension, and the calibrated phase diagrams are *target deliverables* — none are proven or measured yet. This README is written to make that distinction explicit so nothing here is mistaken for a finished claim.

## What it does

- **Endogenous Distribution Learning** — replaces exogenous data `D_{t+1} = P(·|D_t)` with a policy-dependent system `D_{t+1} = T(D_t, π_θ)`.
- **Learned market response operator `T_θ`** — a differentiable, trainable operator over stochastic market transitions, replacing hand-built simulators.
- **Fixed-point objective** — solve `(π*, D*) = argmin_π E_D[R(π)] s.t. D = T(D, π)`.
- **Stability-aware training** — penalizes distribution collapse, liquidity fragmentation, and instability under self-induced market adaptation.
- **Implicit liquidity modeling** — treats liquidity as a latent dynamical field induced by interaction, not an observed variable.

## Core research question

Characterize when the policy/market loop converges to a stable equilibrium vs. fails to converge, as a function of how adversarial the market is.

## Methodology

This section lays out, in order, every component of the research program: the mathematical contributions being targeted, the data this depends on, how that data will be prepared, the model architecture, and how it will be trained and tuned. Each subsection separates **what is established in prior literature** from **what this project must derive, build, or measure**.

### 1. Mathematical contributions

The project's novelty claim is that the performativity stability boundary for a structural market model can be derived analytically from microstructure primitives, rather than assumed or empirically swept. This decomposes into five concrete mathematical targets, ordered by priority.

**1.1 — Analytic stability boundary (Priority 1)**

Target theorem: for an OTC bond market with Guéant–Lehalle–Fernández-Tapia (GLFT) microstructure and Barzykin-style adverse selection, the performativity stability boundary is

```
ε_analytic < γ(k, σ², q) / β(k, A, δ*)
```

where `γ` (strong convexity) and `β` (smoothness) are derived in closed form from the market-making value function — not tuned — and `ε` (feedback sensitivity) is derived from a perturbation expansion around adverse selection, not swept by hand.

- Derive `γ` and `β` from the Avellaneda–Stoikov / GLFT exponential-intensity model `λ(δ) = A·exp(−k·δ)`.
- Derive `dτ/dh` (the toxic-flow feedback slope) from the Barzykin et al. (2025) perturbation expansion, replacing the current heuristic `toxicity_feedback` knob.
- Triangulate `ε` three independent ways — best-response (BR) slope, Sinkhorn/Wasserstein-based estimation, and the Cont–Kukanov–Stoikov informed-flow derivative — and report agreement across all three as the evidentiary bar for the theorem.

**1.2 — Un-blinding the operator: PerfGD correction (Priority 2)**

Target theorem: under GLFT microstructure, a Performative Gradient Descent (PerfGD)–corrected loop, using the analytic `dD/dφ` from 1.1, converges to the performative optimum (PO) at rate `O(1/k)` and remains stable for `ε` beyond the repeated-risk-minimization (RRM) boundary `ε*`.

- Replace blind RRM with PerfGD using the closed-form distribution-response term — no estimation step required once 1.1 is derived.
- Quantify the "echo chamber gap" (stable-vs-optimal spread inflation), expected `O(ε²)` near `ε*`, as a function of `ε`.

**1.3 — Multi-dealer extension and systemic risk (Priority 3)**

Target theorem: for `N` symmetric dealers, the performatively stable Nash equilibrium (PSNE) boundary generalizes to `ε < γ/(N·β)`, with joint effective curvature `γ_joint ≈ γ − N·ε·β`. Market-wide instability — all dealers crossing the boundary simultaneously — emerges at `N·ε·β/γ = 1`.

- Prove existence of the PSNE boundary (via performative games theory) and its convergence rate under joint variational stability (via multiplayer performative prediction theory) for `N`-dealer competition.
- Establish the mean-field limit as `N → ∞`, connecting the discrete `N`-dealer game to a tractable continuum aggregate.

**1.4 — Robust uncertainty quantification (Priority 4)**

Target result: a distributionally robust stable point — the policy stable under worst-case `ε` within an ambiguity set — with nominal boundary `ε*` reported alongside a robust radius `δ` that shrinks at rate `O(1/√n)` in the number of simulated episodes `n`.

- Fit the ambiguity set from cross-seed variance in estimated `ε`.
- Report both nominal and robust stability boundaries; without this, a point estimate of `ε*` is not a statistically defensible claim.

**1.5 — Scale and calibration (Priority 5)**

Target: extend the phase diagram from a single bond/single dealer to 100+ correlated bonds via factor-model dimensionality reduction (Bergault & Guéant), with `γ`/`β` calibrated from real bond characteristics (duration, DV01, spread volatility) or, absent real data, from a synthetic Cont–Kukanov–Stoikov microstructure calibration.

- Derive the dimensionality-reduction error bound as a function of residual factor variance.
- Produce a publication-grade phase diagram with tight median + IQR bands across the bond universe.

### 2. Data collection and potential sources

Two data needs are distinct and should not be conflated: (a) data to calibrate realistic microstructure parameters (`k`, `A`, `σ`, inventory dynamics), and (b) data/simulation needed to empirically measure `ε`, `m`, and the stability boundary across regimes.

**2.1 — Calibration data (real-world microstructure)**

- **TRACE (Trade Reporting and Compliance Engine)** — FINRA's mandatory post-trade reporting feed for U.S. corporate bonds; the standard academic source for OTC bond trade-level data (price, size, timestamp, dealer side). Available via WRDS academic access.
- **MarketAxess / Tradeweb post-trade data** — electronic OTC trading platforms; useful for spread and liquidity metrics on traded bonds, typically licensed/paid access.
- **Bloomberg/Refinitiv terminal data** — bond static data (duration, DV01, credit rating, issue size) needed to calibrate per-bond inventory risk parameters.
- **FINRA/MSRB academic data programs** — possible lower-cost or free academic-tier access to trade-level OTC data; needs direct inquiry, as terms change.
- **Federal Reserve / OFR (Office of Financial Research) corporate bond liquidity datasets** — public aggregate liquidity and spread series useful for sanity-checking calibration, though not trade-level.

**2.2 — Simulation data (synthetic, for stability measurement)**

- Self-generated via the dealer/market simulator: each run logs `(h, τ)` pairs (quote width, realized toxic flow), inventory paths `q_after`, and induced order-flow distributions `D(φ)` across deployments.
- These logs are the raw input to every estimator in 1.1 (BR-slope, Sinkhorn distance, CKS-implied `dλ_informed/dδ`) and must be retained across the full sweep of `ε`, `N` (dealer count), and bond-universe size for the phase diagrams in 1.3–1.5.

**2.3 — Open caveat**

Real trade-level OTC data (TRACE) carries licensing and access lead time; the project should explicitly decide, before experiments begin, whether the headline phase diagrams will be calibrated against real TRACE-derived parameters or run on a fully synthetic, analytically specified microstructure (acceptable for a methodology-first ICAIF/NeurIPS submission, but must be stated plainly rather than implied to be real-market validated).

### 3. Preprocessing

- **Trade data cleaning (if using TRACE or equivalent):** deduplicate reported trades (TRACE reports both sides), filter cancelled/corrected reports, exclude agency trades if isolating principal dealer risk, and align trade timestamps to a common clock across venues.
- **Spread and inventory reconstruction:** derive realized bid-ask half-spreads `h` and inventory paths `q` from sequential trade records per dealer-bond pair; this requires inferring dealer identity and side from TRACE's anonymized-but-attributable fields.
- **Parameter calibration inputs:** compute per-bond volatility `σ`, order-arrival intensity parameters `(A, k)` via maximum-likelihood fit of the exponential-intensity model to inter-trade times, and duration/DV01 from static bond reference data.
- **Simulator log normalization:** standardize logged `(h, τ, q)` tuples across seeds and runs into a consistent schema so that downstream estimators (BR-slope regression, Sinkhorn distance, CKS-implied slope) consume identical input shapes regardless of which sweep produced them.
- **Train/calibration/held-out split for any learned component of `T_θ`:** split simulation episodes (not individual trades, to avoid leakage across a single equilibrium trajectory) into calibration and held-out stability-verification sets.

### 4. Model architecture

- **Market response operator `T_θ`:** a differentiable, parametric operator mapping `(D_t, π_θ)` to `D_{t+1}` — i.e., a learned, trainable substitute for a hand-built market simulator. Architecture choice (e.g., neural SDE, recurrent operator, or attention-based sequence model over order-flow state) is an open design decision to be settled against the stochastic-control baselines below.
- **Baseline structural policy:** the closed-form GLFT/Avellaneda–Stoikov optimal quoting policy, used both as a sanity-check baseline for `T_θ` and as the source of analytically derived `γ`, `β` in 1.1.
- **PerfGD correction module:** a component that estimates or consumes the analytic `dD/dφ` (from 1.1) and feeds a corrected gradient into the policy update — architecturally distinct from blind RRM, which simply retrains on the latest induced distribution.
- **Multi-dealer extension:** `N` instances of the policy/operator pair interacting through a shared induced distribution, required for the systemic-risk experiments in 1.3.
- **Estimator heads (non-trained, diagnostic):** BR-slope regression, Sinkhorn-distance computation (entropic OT), and CKS-implied informed-flow slope — these run alongside `T_θ` as measurement instruments, not as part of the trained model.

### 5. Training and tuning

- **Training loop:** repeated risk minimization (RRM) as the baseline loop — train `π_θ` against the current `D_t`, deploy, observe `D_{t+1} = T_θ(D_t, π_θ)`, repeat — with PerfGD as the corrected variant once 1.1's analytic `dD/dφ` is available.
- **Stability-aware loss terms:** penalties for distribution collapse, liquidity fragmentation, and divergence of the BR-slope modulus `m` away from the contraction regime (`m < 1`), in addition to the base quoting-policy loss.
- **Deploy cadence (greedy vs. lazy):** sweep the number of optimizer steps `K` taken before each redeployment (lazy deploy), since `K` is a stabilization knob — larger `K` reduces the per-update sensitivity the optimizer sees, raising effective curvature `γ_eff = γ − εβ`.
- **Sweep axes for the phase diagram:** feedback gain `ε`, number of dealers `N`, and bond-universe size, each swept across multiple seeds to produce median + IQR bands rather than single-run point estimates.
- **Hyperparameter tuning targets:** learning rate and `K` (lazy-deploy steps) for the RRM/PerfGD loop; entropic regularization strength for the Sinkhorn estimator; ambiguity-set radius for the distributionally robust stability point (1.4).
- **Validation criterion:** agreement between the three independent `ε` estimators (BR-slope, Sinkhorn, CKS-implied) is the primary check that the trained operator and estimators are measuring the same underlying quantity, not an artifact of one estimation method.
